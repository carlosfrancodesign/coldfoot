The GRAMMY Effect™

If we are to believe that Darwinist laws of nature can be applied to sociology, economics, and politics, we may also presume natural selection factors into any competitive market. In the grocery aisle, Sally can choose from six or seven brands of chocolate bar, but Willy Wonka pays a pretty slotting fee to get his chocolate on the shelf so she can see it. The market remains competitive because Willy Wonka is not paying to win, he is simply paying to play, and Sally does not have to buy his chocolate bar. In a free market, they say the best goods and services rise to the top and that competition is better for the consumer because Sally has more options to choose from. Others say this presents her with the illusion of choice.

If the true aim of any serious voting body is to achieve consensus, we may also presume that consensus selects those best suited to represent the will of the majority. An idiocratic voting body such as The Recording Academy© adheres to this principle fully when a minority consortium of card-carrying members (in good dues standing) vote to award the best in Music with the coveted GRAMMY.

Underneath the Dior and coconut oil, artists at the Music Talent Show compete for publicity that leads to a boost in streaming performance, known as The Grammy Effect™. Last year, Billie Eillish saw a 47% boost in streams without winning a single above-the-line category, despite being favored to win Song of the Year. The beauty of this system is that everyone on Grammy Night is a winner except for all of my Parlay Pirates lured into the direct-deposit-sucking storm by that beautiful Half Billie/Half FINNEAS siren call. I firmly believe these mythical demons to be a real and avoidable threat to any young plunderer's booty. But at the start of every Hero's Journey you must first resist the call.

In the tradition of the Greats, I have re-branded a time-tested theory of probability for our current era. Whereas Occam's Razor states that the outcome with the fewest assumptions is the most favorable, Simulation Brain purports the outcome with the fewest assumptions to be the most likely. In other words, the simplest outcome to justify is the most likely to occur. By this logic, it would seemingly behoove us to channel our most ooga-booga tendencies when braving the choppy Polymarket waters. But any old sea dog knows that the more likely the outcome, the less value there is to be gained from risking your hard-earned, dollar-backed Robux. Professional gamblers throw around the v-word in an attempt to legitimize their monetized addiction, but it is nothing more than a pickled red herring aimed at throwing your grubby little nose off the scent of far less sexy, reasonable plays hiding in plain sight. The true alpha lies within this key distinction of the Simulation Brain: if the outcome with the fewest assumptions is the most likely, then the most likely outcome is also the most obvious.
